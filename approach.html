Our Framework

A Cycle, Not a Checklist

The four phases described here are not a linear checklist — they are a recurring cycle. In any sustained engagement, partners will move through these stages multiple times as conditions evolve, evidence accumulates, and understanding deepens. The goal is not to complete a process. It is to build organizations that are consistently better at learning from experience and adjusting accordingly.

Three commitments run through every phase: collaboration (decisions are made with partners, not for them), rigor (methods are appropriate to questions and honest about their limits), and practicality (recommendations must work in the actual context, not a theoretical one).

Evidence to Lead's approach draws explicitly on implementation science — a field that takes seriously the complexity of getting evidence-based practices into routine use inside real organizations. This means attending not just to what should be done, but to the organizational conditions, capacity requirements, and feedback mechanisms that determine whether change actually takes hold.

The Four Phases

Each Phase in Detail

01
Phase 01

Clarify

The single most common source of failed partnerships is misalignment at the start — about what problem is being solved, what success looks like, or what role outside expertise should play. Phase one exists to get honest about all of that.

  • Deep listening with multiple stakeholders — not just leadership
  • Surfacing the implicit assumptions embedded in current strategy
  • Identifying gaps between espoused theory and actual practice
  • Reaching explicit agreement on questions, scope, and decision rights
  • Establishing norms for how uncertainty and disagreement will be handled
Core Principle

Slow down to go fast. Time invested in genuine alignment here prevents far more costly misunderstandings later.

02
Phase 02

Analyze

With a clear question and a shared framework, Evidence to Lead brings analytical rigor to examining the evidence — whether from quantitative data, qualitative fieldwork, or existing research literature. Methods serve the question; they are not selected for their own sake.

  • Designing data collection and analysis that fits the question
  • Bringing relevant external research to bear on the local context
  • Applying methods with transparency about their limits and assumptions
  • Distinguishing what the data shows from what it does not support
  • Iterating findings through dialogue with partners before drawing conclusions
Core Principle

Rigor means being honest about what we know, what we don't, and what the data actually supports — including when findings are inconvenient.

03
Phase 03

Translate

Findings that don't connect to decisions don't improve anything. Translation is the work of making evidence legible and actionable for the specific people in a specific context — without oversimplifying the complexity that actually matters.

  • Developing recommendations grounded in evidence and realistic about context
  • Designing communication strategies for different decision-making audiences
  • Connecting findings to the operational decisions partners actually face
  • Identifying conditions and supports necessary for change to take hold
  • Producing policy-informed products accessible without being dumbed down
Core Principle

The best recommendation is one that can be acted on. Practicality is not a concession to rigor — it is part of what makes the work valuable.

04
Phase 04

Implement

No strategy, however well-designed, survives unchanged contact with reality. The ability to notice what's not working and adjust course quickly is not a sign of weakness — it is the core competency of a learning organization.

  • Building structured learning pauses at key implementation milestones
  • Monitoring leading indicators that can detect drift before it becomes failure
  • Creating organizational conditions for honest, non-defensive review
  • Adapting strategy based on evidence — including changes to assumptions
  • Documenting learning for future cycles and the broader field
Core Principle

Systems improvement is not a destination. The goal is organizations that are consistently better at learning from experience.

Across Every Engagement

Four Standing Commitments

Regardless of phase, scope, or sector — these commitments define how Evidence to Lead shows up in every engagement.

Genuine Collaboration

Decisions are developed with partners, not handed down. Evidence to Lead works as a thought partner — not a vendor delivering a product.

Methodological Rigor

Every analysis is designed with the question in mind and conducted honestly — including clear acknowledgment of what the data does and does not support.

Honest Communication

When evidence is mixed, inconclusive, or contradicts what a partner hoped to find — Evidence to Lead reports that plainly, not packaged to minimize discomfort.

Capacity, Not Dependency

Every engagement is designed to leave the partner more capable — stronger analysis skills, better data routines, and organizational systems that sustain the work.
